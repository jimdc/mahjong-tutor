Glossary (Zero → Confident)

Start with zero knowledge and build up the core ideas, with chess/checkers analogies.

Tile

A tile is like a chess piece or a checker: it’s a basic unit you collect and combine. You do not move tiles on a board; you build patterns in your hand.

Suit (花色 huāsè)

A suit is like a “color” in cards or a set of similar chess pieces. Mahjong has three suits: Dots (筒 tǒng), Bamboo (条 tiáo), and Characters (万 wàn).

Dots are often red/blue (coins), Bamboo are often green (sticks), and Characters are typically red/black with the “wan” character.

Honor Tiles (字牌 zìpái)

Honors are special tiles outside the suits. Think of them like unique pieces in chess (not just another pawn): Winds (风 fēng) and Dragons (箭 jiàn).

Winds: 东南西北 (dōng nán xī běi). Dragons: 中发白 (zhōng fā bái).

  • Winds are directional: East, South, West, North. Some rule sets give bonus value to your seat wind or the prevailing wind.
  • Red Dragon (中 zhōng) often has a red “middle” symbol.
  • Green Dragon (发 fā) is often green and can look like bamboo or a green character.
  • White Dragon (白 bái) is commonly a blank tile or a white frame.

Meld (面子 miànzi)

A meld is a completed pattern of tiles, like forming a line of checkers or a tactical motif in chess. Common melds:

Chow (sequence) (吃 chī)

Pung (triplet) (碰 péng)

Kong (quad) (杠 gàng)

Pair (将 jiàng / 对子 duìzi)

Hand

Your hand is the set of tiles you hold. A winning hand is typically four melds plus one pair. Think of it as “four completed patterns + one double.”

Discard

Each turn you choose one tile to throw away. This is like choosing which chess piece to trade or which checker to sacrifice — you want to keep tiles that build patterns.

Safety (Risk)

Late in a hand, some discards are safer than others. This is like “not hanging your queen.” Safe tiles reduce the chance an opponent wins from your discard.

History & Cultural Context

Mahjong emerged in China in the late 19th century and spread rapidly through East Asia and the world. It blends skill, memory, and probability with a strong social ritual: the table is a place to learn, test intuition, and share time with family and friends.

In many communities, Mahjong is as much about the rhythm of the game as the outcome. The physical tiles, the sound of shuffling, and the pattern recognition all reinforce the game’s identity as tactile and communal.

Different regions shaped Mahjong into distinct styles: Japanese Riichi emphasizes structured scoring patterns (yaku) and visible decision points, Chinese Official focuses on a points threshold with a wide variety of winning patterns, and American Mahjong relies on a yearly card that defines specific hands.

Analogy-wise, Mahjong sits somewhere between chess and poker: like chess, it rewards pattern planning; like poker, it rewards reading opponents and managing risk with incomplete information.
